Mother: He uses Salbutamol inhaler when he gets breathless. But we don't use it regularly.

Doctor: I see. That's actually why he's having these frequent episodes. The preventer needs to be used daily to keep the inflammation down. Let me listen to his chest.
Doctor: Hmm, I can hear bilateral wheezing, more on the right side. His respiratory rate is a bit elevated — about 24 breaths per minute. No chest retractions though, which is good.
Mother: Is it serious, doctor?
Doctor: It's moderate. Not an emergency, but we need to act quickly. I'll start him on Budesonide 200mcg inhaler, one puff twice a day through a spacer. And Salbutamol 2 puffs every 4 hours if he's wheezy.
Mother: A spacer? What is that?
Doctor: It's a tube that attaches to the inhaler. It helps the medicine reach his lungs properly instead of just hitting the back of his throat. Much more effective for children.
Mother: Oh, okay. Where do I get that?
Doctor: Any medical shop will have it. I'll also give him a short course of Prednisolone syrup — 15mg once daily for 5 days — to bring down the acute inflammation.
Mother: Any side effects of the steroid, doctor?
Doctor: Short courses are safe. He might be a bit hyper or have trouble sleeping. Give the syrup in the morning.
Mother: Alright, doctor.
Doctor: Keep him away from dust, smoke, and pets if possible. Make sure the house is well-ventilated. And please, use the preventer inhaler every day — don't stop it even when he feels better.
Mother: I understand now, doctor. I'll be more careful this time.
Doctor: Follow up in 2 weeks. If he has severe breathing difficulty, blue lips, or can't speak in full sentences, go to the emergency room immediately.
